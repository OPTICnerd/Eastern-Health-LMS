TIC Practice #1: Provide a Safe Environment for the Trauma Survivor

Schachter et al.87 describe nine principles of sensitive practice that can be used to foster feelings of safety for the trauma survivor:

Principle Details

Respect

The attitude and interactions of the physician and support staff should convey respect for patient. Respect involves recognizing and accommodating the individuality of the patient.

Rapport

Developing rapport with a patient requires making the patient feel comfortable and involves engaging in a way that conveys respect and empathy.

Taking time

This can be challenging in a busy doctor’s office. However, taking a moment to listen empathically may be all that is required for the patient to feel safe and able to engage and be fully present in the healthcare encounter. Unfortunately, lack of time is commonly cited as a barrier to providing trauma-informed care.21,7

Sharing information

Inform the patient about what to expect in the appointment or a procedure and provide the rationale for the approach or procedure being undertaken. This empowers the patient and conveys respect.

Sharing control

Include the patient in decision-making when appropriate. Give the patient choice whenever possible. Clarify the roles and responsibilities of the physician and the patient, recognizing that each has a unique and important role to play in the doctor/patient relationship. Establishing a collaborative relationship where each has an important role to play empowers the patient and is often in sharp contrast to the lack of control experienced when traumatized.

Respecting boundaries

Personal boundaries were violated for patients who suffered interpersonal trauma. As a result, boundary crossings can be major triggers for traumatized patients. Personal boundaries include both physical and psychological boundaries. Healthcare inevitably involves bumping up against the personal boundaries of patients. Seeking permission and explaining the need for boundary crossing (e.g., touch, personal questions) will mitigate if not eliminate any potential discomfort.

Understanding nonlinear healing

It is important to recognize that a patient’s ability to engage in healthcare may vary from visit to visit. What a patient may be able to tolerate at one appointment might be too difficult at the next. This means that it is important to check in with the patient at every encounter and accommodate as needed.

Fostering mutual learning

Because many trauma survivors have not experienced a sense of safety as children, they may only be learning about it in adulthood. As a result, survivors may need encouragement to become full, active participants in their own health care.

Demonstrating an understanding of interpersonal violence

It is reassuring for a traumatized patient to know that their health care provider has an understanding of the prevalence and impact of interpersonal trauma. This can be reflected in such things as having pamphlets on the topic in the waiting room, being willing to ask about trauma, and engaging with the patient in a trauma-sensitive way.
