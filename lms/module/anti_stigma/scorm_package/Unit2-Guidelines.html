Phase Description

Phase 1: Safety and Stabilization

Phase 1 of treatment is the “safety and stabilization phase” where the patient and therapist work collaboratively to help the patient establish internal and external safety in their lives and stabilization of their symptoms, including hyperarousal, hypoarousal, poor sleep, interpersonal difficulties, affect instability, and in some cases, their chronic self-harm behaviors or suicidality. Phase 1 treatment addresses internal and external threat. The focus is on safety planning and developing internal and external resources to help the patient attain and maintain safety and stability in their lives. Success in establishing safety and stability will partly depend on the extent to which this effort is supported or impeded by the patient’s environment. Improving sleep, which helps increase internal stability and decrease other symptoms, may be one goal of phase 1 treatment. Psychoeducation about trauma and its effects is also an important component of phase 1 treatment. For some patients with Complex PTSD (or dissociative identity disorder), it may be necessary to remain in phase 1 of treatment indefinitely.

Phase 2: Working with Memories
Phase 2 of treatment, sometimes called “remembering and mourning”, or the “exposure phase” involves discussing and processing traumatic memories. It is appropriate to start phase 2 when the patient is sufficiently safe and stable. Processing traumatic memories can be destabilizing and thus the processing of traumatic memories should be done with a therapist experienced in treating trauma survivors. If the patient becomes too destabilized, it may be necessary to return to phase 1 treatment to restabilize before continuing. In phase 2 of treatment, patients will, in the context of the relationship with their therapist, remember, talk about, and work through their traumatic memories, and challenge their trauma-based beliefs about themselves, other people, and the world. Exposure therapy often involves graduated exposure to the traumatic memories (i.e., starting with the least distressing memory rather than the most distressing memory of trauma).26

Phase 3: Reintegration
Phase 3 of treatment, often called the “reintegration phase”, involves patients taking the new skills and beliefs about themselves and the world, and trying them out. This tends to be a much more practical stage, where patients practice what they have learned, and in this way, further challenge their trauma-engendered beliefs and attitudes, and replace these beliefs with a more realistic, and hopeful understanding of themselves and their environment. Patients work towards consolidating their treatment gains and applying what they have learned to their work lives, relationships and community.
