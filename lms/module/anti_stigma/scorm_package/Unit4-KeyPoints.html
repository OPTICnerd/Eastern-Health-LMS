Key Points

  • PTSD is a highly comorbid condition. Therefore when the clinician identifies symptoms of PTSD in a patient, it is important to actively look for criteria of other psychiatric diagnoses, especially Major Depression and Alcohol/Substance Use Disorders.
  • PTSD is often a chronic and disabling condition. Sometimes it takes years for a person to present for care for their symptoms.
  • PTSD can affect all areas of a person’s life. Therefore it is important to get a comprehensive psycho-social history from these patients.
  • There is a significant association between PTSD and suicidality. The presence of comorbid Major Depression compounds this risk. Therefore, all patients with symptoms of PTSD and/or Major Depression should be routinely assessed for suicidality.
  • Treatment can be effective in relieving the symptoms of PTSD. There are effective pharmacologic and psychotherapeutic treatments available. Treatment of PTSD should use a phase-oriented, trauma-focused approach.
  • For those suffering from Complex PTSD, treatments which have shown efficacy for Simple PTSD may not be as effective. Treatment for Complex PTSD generally needs to be of longer duration, and comprehensive, using several approaches to decrease symptoms and suffering. Sometimes, a “team” of clinicians (2 or more who are communicating and co-ordinating their care of the patient) is necessary to provide optimal treatment for the person with Complex PTSD. Those with the most chronic symptoms of Complex PTSD may require years of treatment, intermittently over a lifetime.
  • As a primary care physician, you can make an important difference and support your patient’s recovery from PTSD. Applying the principles of trauma-informed care as outlined in this course and, if possible, taking time to talk to your patient about whatever is on their mind can make a difference.
