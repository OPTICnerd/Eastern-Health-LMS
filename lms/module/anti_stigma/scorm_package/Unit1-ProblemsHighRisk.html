Suicidal behavior

Patients with PTSD may think about suicide frequently, sometimes every day. This is not uncommon for patients with such a history. Research has shown a positive association between PTSD and suicidality, and this association is compounded when major depression is also present.76 The fact that the patient has stated that they think about suicide may or may not be indicative of a risk for suicide. To further complicate the picture, although non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) is not a suicide attempt, there is an increased risk for suicide among those who engage in NSSI. Research has found the 50-70% of adolescents who self-injured reported at least one previous suicide attempt70,72. The method of self-injury may assist in identifying those at greater risk of suicide.8 The more dangerous the method of self-injury, the more intensive the assessment should be. Thus, a risk assessment is needed for medical, ethical and legal reasons. See TIC Practice #7: Recognize Need for Immediate Intervention or Referral Based on Risk for more information on assessing risk.

Suicidality in the Veteran

Those who have served in the Canadian Armed Forces may be reluctant to disclose, or may not realize that they have a mental health problem.38,107 As well, military training confers a professional and disciplined demeanor on those who have served. Because of this, veterans and reservists can appear more healthy and “together” than they actually are. Research has shown that there is an association between the presence of PTSD and suicidality. Comorbid depression further compounds the risk for suicide in this population.76 This means the family physician must be vigilant when assessing the psychological state of these patients. Identifying mental disorders such as PTSD, major depression and substance use disorders is the first step in assessing veterans and reserve force members for suicidality. Those who screen positively for a mental disorder should always be assessed for suicidal ideation including intent, plan, lethality of the plan, and access to lethal means (eg firearms, stockpile of medications). Based on this assessment, appropriate action must be taken by the family physician, and the patient must be monitored until he/she is no longer at imminent risk.

Mental disorders, and precipitating crises should be attended to, and family, friends and other support groups should be enlisted to help the patient. In one U.S. study, only 30% of veterans with a diagnosis of depression who died by suicide had documented evidence of being asked about suicide on their last visit to their physician.93 This highlights the importance and challenges of assessing for suicidality in the veteran population. Members of other “paramilitary” professions, such as police officers and firefighters, may present with similar clinical profiles and risks for suicide.

Non-suicidal self-harm behaviour

There are a range of reasons for why people self-harm.57 Affect regulation is the most common. Self-injury can be a way to manage intense and overwhelming negative feelings. Relief or calm often follows self-injury.56 Self-injury can also be a way of punishing the self. Less common is that it is an attempt to influence someone else (e.g., to garner affection). It can be used as a way to manage dissociative symptoms such as feeling numb or unreal. Paradoxically, it can be used as a way to resist urges to attempt suicide. Some might use it to experience excitement. Self harm can include such behaviors as trichotillomania (hair pulling), injuring the skin (e.g., cutting, skin picking, pinching, hitting), injurious masturbation, binge eating, purging, etc.

Interpersonal problems

The experience of complex trauma leads to difficulties with in relationships.26,46 This can include difficulties trusting others, withdrawal from others, a preoccupation with finding someone to “rescue” them, vacillating between approach and avoidance of relationships, and an inability to maintain healthy boundaries. Sexual revictimization has been shown to be associated with interpersonal dysfunction.110,111

Revictimization

There is a large body of research that shows a relationship between psychological trauma and revictimization, especially sexual revictimization.112 PTSD symptoms are a risk factor for revictimization.59 Reexperiencing symptoms were shown to predict subsequent interpersonal violence by a nonintimate perpetrator and hyperarousal symptoms predicted exposure to other traumatic stressors.25

Sexual issues

Sexual issues are common among survivors of sexual assault and childhood sexual abuse. Responses can range from being phobic of sex to being hypersexual. Sexual phobia can be expressed in an avoidance of sexual relationships altogether or an aversion to specific sexual activities.51 Among both men and women, a history of childhood sexual abuse has been associated with sexual risk behaviours including sex trading, more sexual partners, and earlier age of first intercourse.89 Sexual dysfunction can develop for women who experienced sexual traumas includeing dissatisfaction, as well as problems with sexual motivation, arousal, orgasm, vaginismus and dyspareunia.51

Aggressive behaviour

One of the diagnostic criteria for PTSD is irritable or angry outbursts with little or no provocation.2 According to a study of US military personnel, anger, aggression and hostility occur more often in those with PTSD compared to military personnel without the disorder.50 Problems with angry feelings and regulation of this affect also occur in civilians with PTSD.
