Overview

Now that we have examined the potential for stigma to negatively affect both a patient’s health and the delivery of care, we are left with the question of how to enhance skills to support care delivery so that stigma is recognized and reduced.

 

As previous materials and videos have shown, mental illness has neurobiological underpinnings. It’s treatment - including medications AND talking therapies like CBT and IPT and psychodynamic therapy â€“ has neurobiological underpinnings (Fuchs, 2004).

 

Talking with patients, listening to them, hearing them, sharing problem solving with them, not only goes a long way to relieve symptoms of mental illness, and may be largely responsible for the placebo effect, but also significantly fights stigma. This is due to the fact that good communication diminishes social distance and creates a situation where healthy social contact is occurring. This is the link between enhancing skills and stigma, similar to using the process of integration to deal with racial prejudice. You could do a workshop with social contact which would reduce prejudice, but by establishing a process such as not allowing schools to segregate which facilitates contact you may be creating an ongoing positive process. Thus we will present a skill set that may put such a process in action.
