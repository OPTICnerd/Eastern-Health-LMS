Emerging Concepts of Stigma and Disability Policy

Emerging Concepts of Stigma and Disability-Social Science Research
  • A Social Problem beyond the individual- Less a personal impairment of the individual (Goffman) which risks biologic reductionism and prospective prognostic negativism, but instead more a characteristic of the social group, their attitudes and social context. Stigma as a social problem and as social discrimination.
  • Social Justice Model-Movements on autonomy and human rights, civil rights and a right to health (UN convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2007), Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms (1982).
  • Power Imbalance; “Stigma is entirely dependent on social, economic, and political Power”, with the stigmatized having less or losing power. “In many instance power differences are so taken for granted as to seem unproblematic.” (Link and Phelan, p.375, 2001)
  • Recovery and Hope — As stigma is increasingly perceived as a social problem, individuals have autonomy, responsibility, and participation towards their own recovery.

Table 1 Historical Concepts of Stigma and Disability Policy

Evolution of Concepts of Stigma and Disability Policy Development (Jongbloed, 1985)

  1. Law and Order Approach — 1800’s-Behaviour of people with mental illness and intellectual impairments were dangerous and disruptive and a threat to law and order. They were institutionalized to protect themselves and societies from them. Asylum model 1860-1890.
  2. Medical and Economic Models — 1920-1970’s-disability is an attribute of a person who is functionally limited and biologically inferior (Hahn). Unemployment is due to personal deficit and rehabilitation is aimed to return the individual to work as soon as possible.
  3. Sociopolitical Model — 1970’s-disability stems not from individual limitations but from the failure of the social environment to adjust to the needs of people with different abilities (Hahn).
