TIC Practice #6: Recognize Signs and Symptoms of Current Self-harm

Self-injury can be one of the most troubling symptoms for health providers to recognize, understand and respond to. Research on the attitudes of healthcare providers towards those who self-injure suggests that irritation and anger are common reactions of healthcare providers in general medical settings.86 A lack of confidence in knowing how to deal with patients who self-injure is a common problem.42 However, training on the topic can lead to improved attitudes and confidence.86

 

Deliberate self-injury can take a variety of forms, such as, cutting, hair pulling, scratching/picking, self poisoning, hanging, asphyxiation with CO/other gas, bruising, burning, etc. The most common forms of self-injury are cutting, scratching, bruising and burning. Typically, patients do not go to their doctor to discuss their self harm. However, the self harm may be so physically damaging that they require health care, such as needing stitches for a deep cut or antibiotics for an infected wound. At other times, the patient may be seeking care for an unrelated health concern but they present in such a way that deliberate self harm is suspected. For example, there may be bruises on the body, evidence of a recent wound, unusual scarring, etc.

Recommendations Details

Determine intent

When deliberate self-injury is detected, the first step is to determine whether the behavior was an instance of non-suicidal self-injury or whether it was a genuine suicide attempt. The physician might simply and compassionately ask, “Was this a suicide attempt?” Discuss the intent of the act with the patient.

Assess level of risk

Regardless of intent, it is also important to assess the degree of self harm or the danger associated with the particular form of self-harm. If there is grave danger associated with the self-harm, the physician should convey concern and provide information about the risk and avoid expressions of judgment or alarm.


Although non-suicidal self-injury is not a suicide attempt, there is an increased risk for suicide among those who engage in non-suicidal self-injury. Research has found the 50-70% of adolescents who self-injured reported at least one previous suicide attempt.70,72 The method of self-injury may assist in identifying those at greater risk of suicide.8 The more dangerous the method of self-injury, the more thorough the assessment should be.

Determine the function of self-injury

With a respectful curiosity, the physician can ask, “What does the self-injury do for you?” The most common answer will be that it helps to manage the intense emotional distress. Knowing the function of the self-harm, the physician is in a position to help the survivor consider other ways to satisfy that need.


There are a range of reasons for why people self-injure.57 Affect regulation is the most common. Self-injury can be a way to manage intense and overwhelming negative feelings. Relief or calm often follows self-injury.56 Self-injury can be a way of punishing the self. Less common is that it is an attempt to influence someone else (e.g., to garner affection). It can be used as a way to manage dissociative symptoms such as feeling numb or unreal. Paradoxically, it can be used as a way to resist urges to attempt suicide. Some might use it to experience excitement.

Take a history of self-injury

Gather information about the history of self-injury as well as the current Episode. Ask how long they have engaged in this or other self-harm behaviors, the frequency of self-harm behaviours, how long the episodes last, and the damage inflicted. It is also important to understand the context in which the self-harm occurs and the triggers to self-harm. By understanding the triggers to self-harm, this can provide some clues as to strategies for avoiding self-harm.

Recognize who is most likely to self-injure

Individuals who engage in self-injury have been found to have a number of psychological characteristics.57 They suffer more intense and frequent negative emotions. They lack skills to manage their negative emotions, may have less awareness of their emotions or may use dissociation as a way to cope with intense negative affect. They are often highly self-critical and can feel intense anger towards themselves.

Validate that self-injury is a coping strategy

There is often a lot of shame is response to having self-harmed. The physician should convey an understanding that that self-injury is a coping strategy, a way to manage intense and difficult feelings. Self-injury is the best coping strategy that the patient knows for managing intense and overwhelming negative emotion.

Discuss other options for coping

In a nonjudgmental way, open up a conversation to discuss other options that the patient could consider for dealing with these difficult feelings. It is important to recognize that other, more benign strategies for coping will not give as immediate and complete a relief as the self-harm. The goal is to reduce self-injury and eventually replace it with more adaptive coping strategies. The patient should be encouraged to come up with a list of alternative coping strategies that she can try first before she resorts to the self-harm. Even delaying self-injury while trying out alternative coping strategies can be helpful by allowing some time to pass and the intensity of the negative emotion to lessen; then, if the patient can no longer resist the self-harm, it is likely to be less extreme.


Helping the patient to come up with their own list is likely to be more effective. At the same time, providing the patient with some ideas can stimulate the patient’s creative thinking. As always, a collaborative approach to generating the list is ideal. Alternative behaviours can include activities to symbolize the act, distraction, physical activities, delaying the behaviour, social support, imagery, art and writing. Some specific ideas for dealing with the urge to self-harm include: wait 15 minutes, do mindful diaphragmatic breathing, call a friend, do strenuous exercise to raise your heart rate, put your hand in a bucket of ice, snap an elastic band on your wrist, punch a pillow, take a hot bath or shower.


Inform the patient that alternatives to self-injury are usually not as effective in the short-term. However, the goal is that with practice and over time, these strategies will serve the function that the self-injury behaviour accomplished.

Refer for counseling/therapy, provide information on hotlines

Self-injurious behaviour is an indication that the patient is struggling to cope and would benefit from additional support.
