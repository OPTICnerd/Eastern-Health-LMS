Complex Trauma and Complex PTSD

Many traumatic experiences are circumscribed events that are of time-limited duration. However, in some cases people experience complex trauma, which is chronic, unremitting trauma that continues or repeats over time. This type of trauma is particularly damaging to the individual’s mental health and development, especially if it occurred during childhood, and is often referred to as Complex PTSD. Adults might develop symptoms of Complex PTSD from a complex trauma experienced in adulthood; these are events that are especially extreme such as genocide. People who experience complex trauma often report additional symptoms alongside formal PTSD symptoms. Although the DSM-5 does not have a diagnostic category specific to complex trauma, the symptoms of complex trauma have been referred to as Complex PTSD, or Disorders of Extreme Stress Not Otherwise Specified (DESNOS).23,45,103

Symptom Details

Emotional Dysregulation

May include persistent sadness, suicidal thoughts, explosive anger, or inhibited anger.

Pathological Alterations in Consciousness

Includes forgetting traumatic events, reliving traumatic events, or having episodes in which one feels detached from one's mental processes or body (dissociation).

Distorted Self-Perception

May include helplessness, shame, guilt, stigma, and a sense of being completely different from other human beings

Distorted Perceptions of the Perpetrator

Examples include attributing total power to the perpetrator, becoming preoccupied with the relationship to the perpetrator, or preoccupied with revenge.

Dysfunctional Relations with Others

Examples include isolation, distrust, being overly submissive, or a repeated search for a rescuer.

Pathological Alterations in One's System of Meaning

May include a loss of sustaining faith or a sense of hopelessness and despair.
