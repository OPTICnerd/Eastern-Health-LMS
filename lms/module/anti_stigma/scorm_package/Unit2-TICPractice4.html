TIC Practice #4: Recognize That Some Health Practices Can Be Traumatizing

It is important to recognize that almost anything can be triggering for a patient with a trauma history. It is impossible to anticipate every possible trigger because each trauma survivor has had a unique experience and often triggers are unique to their experience. At the same time, there are some health practices where there is a greater likelihood of the practice being triggering and can even lead to an experience of retraumatization. Some obvious examples within primary care include pelvic examinations, rectal exams, and breast exams.

Recommendations Details

Ask if patient has ever had difficulties with this type of procedure

See Handbook on sensitive practice, page 33, table 2.

If yes, ask “what can I do to make it easier?”

Explain procedure

There are two levels of explanation. One is to give an overall description of the procedure before you even begin. Once beginning the procedure, explain each step of what you are doing and, if possible why you are doing it, before you actually do it. This removes the element of surprise and allows the patient an opportunity to be mentally prepared.

Collaborate on how to reduce risk for traumatization

Once the procedure has been described and before beginning the actual procedure, it can be helpful to discuss how to reduce risk for retraumatization. Consider possible ways in which the patient might exercise some control over the situation

Provide choice where possible

The patient might have their own ideas about what might make the procedure easier to undergo or you might be able to provide some suggestions.


For example:


“Would it be helpful to have a mirror so that you can see what I am doing?”

Or

“Would you like to insert the speculum yourself?”

Or

“Do you want to give me a signal if you need me to stop the procedure?”

Ask permission at each step of procedure

It can be helpful to ask permission, such as “Is that ok?” as the procedure progresses.

Demonstrate before doing

Wherever possible demonstrate to the patient what you will be doing. This again reduces the element of surprise and allows the patient to mentally prepare. It is also possible for the patient to anticipate if some part of the procedure will be triggering and this can open up a discussion about how to mitigate that response.

Check-in with patient

As the procedure progresses, ask how the patient is doing. “Are you ok?” “How are you doing so far?” If there is no response, this may mean the patient has been triggered and is dissociating. At that point, it can be helpful to remind the patient who you are, that they are in your office and having a particular procedure. Ask the patient if they can hear you and is with you. If it is not possible to stop the procedure, let the patient know when it will be completed and continue to talk in a reassuring manner. Take time after the procedure to ensure the patient is present and feels safe or has a plan to take care of themselves after they leave your office.

Normalize reactions

Let your patient know that their reactions make sense given their trauma history.
